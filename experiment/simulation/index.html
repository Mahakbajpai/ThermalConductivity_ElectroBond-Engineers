Thermal Conductivity Calculation

The thermal conductivity (k) is calculated using Fourier's Law:

k = (Q × d) / (A × ΔT)

Where:

  • Q = Heat transfer rate (W)
  • d = Distance between plates (m)
  • A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
  • ΔT = Temperature difference (°C or K)

Theory

Thermal conductivity measures a material's ability to conduct heat. In this experiment, we measure how heat transfers between plates through different liquids, with the hot plate cooling down and cold plate warming up over time.

Thermal Conductivity Theory

What is Thermal Conductivity?

Thermal conductivity (often denoted by k, λ, or κ) is the property of a material to conduct heat. It is evaluated primarily in terms of Fourier's Law for heat conduction. Heat transfer occurs at a higher rate across materials of high thermal conductivity than across materials of low thermal conductivity.

Measurement Principle

In this virtual experiment, we use a simple setup to measure the thermal conductivity of liquids:

  • A liquid is placed between two plates at different temperatures
  • The hot plate starts at a high temperature and cools down
  • The cold plate starts at a low temperature and warms up
  • We measure how the temperatures change over time
  • From this temperature change, we can calculate the thermal conductivity

Key Equations

The fundamental equation for heat conduction is Fourier's Law:

Q = -k·A·(ΔT/d)

Where:

  • Q = Heat transfer rate (W)
  • k = Thermal conductivity (W/m·K)
  • A = Cross-sectional area (m²)
  • ΔT = Temperature difference (K or °C)
  • d = Distance between plates (m)

We can rearrange this to solve for k:

k = (Q × d) / (A × ΔT)

Factors Affecting Thermal Conductivity

Several factors influence the thermal conductivity of liquids:

  • Temperature: Generally decreases with increasing temperature
  • Density: More dense liquids often have higher conductivity
  • Molecular Structure: Complex molecules tend to have lower conductivity
  • Presence of Impurities: Can either increase or decrease conductivity
